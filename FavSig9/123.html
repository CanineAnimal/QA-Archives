No, the Roman Empire certainly would not have fully industrialised for at least another millennium. Industrialisation would have taken a complete re-wiring of an agricultural society, which would certainly take centuries to do. And while you do need global (free) trade, a capitalist system, and incentive for more efficient production to industrialise, any competent society can provide incentive. Global trade I don't see as a problem for Rome (Roman coins have been found in ancient India). The major problem would be capitalism (or rather, the invention of it). Rome would have to greatly liberalise in order for economists to draw up another (more effective) economic system. But I see it as undeniable that Rome was much more than a Mars-fuelled war machine, intent on expansion. The collapse of Rome was consequential, and it did de-evolve technology. People (in cities) went from having sewer systems and central heating to living in straw houses. And that's only surface-level de-evolution. I think it's ridiculous to say that going from a vast Empire to various city-states is not that big of a deal. And most medieval technological advancements were made out of need. Those that weren't, were made due to liberalisation, something that would happen even in the Roman Empire, as religion took the centre stage, and authoritarian warlords became less prevalent. And a great deal of them came from Iberia under (relatively free) Muslim rule, during the Golden Age of Islam. The rest of Europe then took after Spain and Portugal. And as that led to the Modern Era, Britain, who had disavowed the sole right of kings to rule, was in the perfect condition to encourage mercantilism, free trade, and free thought - which led to capitalism, innovation, and industrialisation. To say this could not have happened in Rome can seem tempting, but even Rome had memory of the Republic. In the end, though most of this is speculation, I stand by my "myth". Not in 1190, not in 1500, but certainly before 1780.
