It's funny, really. Most of my detractors would expect me to loathe this man for the economic system he represents. Thereby entirely missing the point that while I may have my personal qualms with capitalism as a system, I have no inherent hatred for those who employ it to their own ends. Perhaps, in some small way, it does sour my opinions - but to have international relations ruled by such a minor thing as personal preference would be idiotic. Now, onto the man himself. Our disagreements in economics aside, there are quite a few points surrounding Gallywix that I find rather pleasing - his apparent devotion to the protection of the individual, cultural acceptance and social liberalism all ring true with my own tenets. Admittedly, we operate by entirely different mechanisms - his insistence on the creation of a pseudo-monarchy is a baffling one, and indeed the stock he seems to put in inheritable rule is seemingly nonsensical for a man of his origins and stature. And then there is his policy of child labour - perhaps one of the most grievous manifestations of capitalistic excess and prioritization of economic efficiency above all else. It seems to go entirely against the apparent care he exhibits for his citizens, and thereby I simply fail to comprehend its efficacy in his doctrine - unless, of course, our definitions of child labour are wholly different. The description I was given is somewhat... inadequate in such regards. Informational dearth aside, I still hold a mildly positive opinion of the Trade Prince. By no means is this a wholehearted endorsement - several aspects of his policy still irk me to no end. But he is far better than the tinpot dictators, the totalitarian sockpuppets of so many bloated regimes the world over. We even agree on an anti-democratic stance, of a sort - while I find his de-facto merger of the industry and state and subsequent insistence that it is the industrial concerns, and not the state, which bear an influence on the people rather hypocritical, we share a conviction in the need for some guiding framework for the purposes of national stability. Ironically enough, his opinion of me must be less than stellar. But then, perhaps he has sufficient sense to evaluate foreign politics on something more than a surface level. Time will tell, I suppose.
