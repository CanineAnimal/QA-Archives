ANATOLY
THE PARAMILITARY REGIONS
“ I have not done one of these in a long time. Have I? No, I have not. Who is it then? Kai Tsofan. I have seen that name before, while reading Inogo's information sheet. Well, I do not know how this letter is going to reach him. He is dead after all, is he not? Then again, how do people even receive what we send? It is better I leave that for another time. For now, well, it is time to talk about you.

So, Tsofan, may I say that you had a rather peaceful early life? Helping your family out. Reading and even composing literature and art. Gaining more knowledge on the world. Having the means to expand upon it. While I would say that learning combat skills is not exactly ideal, I have to admit: They are useful to have. I have a feeling we both wish they were not. But reality is reality, is it not? Regardless... may your brother rest in peace. And in Heaven, or what it would be in your faith. I have a feeling he was a good man. Maybe even one of the best for your family. On a lighter note, I do find it rather inspiring to see you taking up the mantle on his behalf. The fact you managed to upkeep your family during its toughest times says a lot. A lot about your virtues, of course.

...I see an... unnerving parallel between us both. We were both conscripted. Granted, in your case it was different. I was conscripted during peacetime, and then sent as a reservist. You were conscripted directly into the war. But the states we were conscripted were a bit similar in their goals. At least, as far as I know. I have a feeling the consequences for refusing the call were the same. What was different, though, was our responses. I complied up until... Kashmir, was it? You, however, decided to respond from the first moment. By sparking up a rebellion. A very brutal one, in fact. I... want to believe that this was the result of not having any other possible choice. But I am not sure whether to condemn it for its brutality. Or to commend it for its light-hearted intentions. After all, it is one thing to refuse it when your homeland is being attacked. It is another to do it when your homeland is the one attacking others.

It would also be here where you would put your plans in action. Your plans to create a prosperous and united Sherechia. To crash down the old tribal system and replace it with a republic where every tribe would be autonomous. I now wonder, what would have happened if you had not needed their support? Would you still have given them autonomy? Or would you have not cared for them, and, instead, have yours be superior? I do want to think that it is the former, of course. But this hypothetical does have quite a bit of food for thought. Does it not? Regardless, after years of brutal fighting, you would finally see your dream come true. And, to celebrate, you would let its new citizens decide whether they wanted to keep you in. I wonder, what was that process like? I know you mention parties, but... I am not familiar with things like these. Apologies for the questions.

After retiring due to health issues, you would live a peaceful life until 1965. Of course, this death... could it be called that? Knowing what happened after - coming back as an archangel of sorts to Inogo - makes me doubt whether it really was a death. Maybe it was a physical death. But not a death of the soul. I know the afterlife exists in my faith, Christianity. I do not think those in the afterlife can interact with those on the mortal realm. Maybe it is different over there in Sherechia?

I think it is only fair that I do analyze what I can of your leadership. First things first, I do think that it is inspiring that you did indeed decide to chase your dreams. Even more so the fact that most of it was done via pure diplomacy. Barring the force that was carried out against the Latsande warlords. Then again... were they ever willing to use words? Although, doing it after the war... I think it is a bit too excessive. Couple that with the widespread corruption, and I have a feeling you were not that competent. At least not as a peacetime leader. Maybe as a revolutionary you were indeed competent. It is hard to judge. I am not experienced in leadership in general. Much less one that is similar to yours.

Since it looks like I can not say much else, let me say this: May you rest in peace. And may Inogo learn the lessons you never had the chance to learn. Farewell, Kai Tsofan, farewell. ”
