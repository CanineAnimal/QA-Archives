Melding[1] on His Caserly[2] Majesty, Greatcaser[3], Drighten[4], “Charles von Roland”
If I may be soothfast[5], I know not whether to laugh at or be angry with this man. On one hand, his biography reads like a novel so much that I feel a bayonet is stabbing deeper and deeper into my skull as I take these words as they are written. On the other, I feel as if my pride as an officer is being trampled upon as I read on this man’s unwise deeds in the slaughtfield[6] taken to be byspels[7] of moudiness[8] for his soldiers and burghers[9] to follow.

Born in the purple, this man had his life set from the start, if only he had done nothing but sit and smile for the bilthboxes[10]. However, either he or his father—Caser[11] Fernando, bearing a Spanish name, notwithstanding being either a Swede or an Engle[12]—chose to besooth[13] him as a meanling[14] so he may go to school “without lovelingism[15] and overmety[16] pampering and care”. Do princes and princesses in this world not have teachers hired by the caserhouse[17]? And for this, he was pestered[18] and shunned by his fellow children—I could not help but laugh at the drama of it all as I wrote this.

Shortly after bytreading[19] the military, this “Imperium” boded crig[20] upon Russland[21] after she overfell[22] some worthless breakaway state in Easteuropa[23]. Now, I may have fought the Russers[24] myself, but I know nothing of the geopolitic of this time or world, so I cannot say anything on the soundness of this beslotting[25]. What I could say, however, is that his father was either as mindless as his son or was plotting to have him killed, sending Charles straight to the frontlines. I know not what rank he held at first, but seeing as it was weighty[26] enough for him to have led a “stour[27] craft[28] of the forehode[29]”, he, by any and all means, should not have been fighting in the thick of the stride[30], besunderly[31] in storming Sanct Petersburg, one of, if not the most, strategish[32] steads[33] for the Russish[34] leadership. It comes as a shock to no one that he was heavily wounded, but it comes as a shock to myself that he has healed from each and every one of these wounds without so much as a narr[35], given how this seems to be something that happens daily for the earm[36] Prince.

He later fought in this so-called “Koenigsburg”—I onnim[37] this to be a misspelling of Königsberg—and then Warsaw, Narva, Helsing[38], Riga, Wilna[39], and lastly, Reval[40]. In every slaught[41], he was shot, bruised, blown up, or burnt one way or another, but somehow—and unluckily for him—lived through what I could only bewrite[42] as an orfaring[43] I would not wish upon my worst foe. Or should I say luckily for him? Since it seems that Charles here is asking at every turn for the Russers to kill him; for byspel, in the Slaught of Warsaw, he seemed to fancy himself a new Napoleon and took nothing but a sabre and pistol while leading at the forefront of an ongripe[44] on the Russish lines—if I were a Russish thane[45], I would have befealened[46] my men to fire first and foremost at the mad officer heading straight toward us with a sword. He seems to not understand that commanders are always on the fiend’s[47] crosshairs, and that if a general were to fall in any stride, his men would be running around like headless chickens and the whole slaught would have been made pointless. “Undying[48] Soldier” indeed he may be, but even if a thin line sunders[49] moudiness and thoughtlessness, he is fastly[50] within the latter.

As Caser, his rule seems to be unnoteworthy, though he seems to style himself as a “man of the folk[51]”. After bewedding[52] this “Grace” woman, whom I will not speak further about, Caser Charles began a cleansing of his regering[53] and bureaucracy, akin to John the Fearsome* and his own cleansing of the Russish aristocracy. I would like to point out that his crowning[54] happened forthwith[55] on the day of his father’s death and thus the start of Charles’s own rule, so I cannot help but think that, like his father plotting Charles’s death in crig, he in turn plotted his father’s death—only this time, Charles had his way. Though, I must say, I have neither the time nor the gethuld[56] nor even the smallest sort of love for politicking, so, in a way, he is a better statesman than I could ever hope to be—not that it makes him much of a better officer or husband. However, notwithstanding how hard one fands[57] to do so, it is unmaily[58] to ever fully root out the rot[59] in any system, unless this man could somehow orset[60] menish[61] beings with something such as bees, whose whole dull and meaningless lives are besundered[62] for the hive and nothing else.

To keep things short: Charles is an odd fellow. I have a lot of aught[63] for Casers, but not so much for him. As an officer, he gives his deeds little thought. As a ruler, he is as ruthless as he is naïve. As a man, he is a narcissistish[64] egoist who foregives[65] to be a selfless wellwiller[66]. Shame that Lady Luck, or likely that scoundrel we call “God”, blessed only him and no one else in all of Swedeland-England[67].

That is all.

Underwritten[68],



Günther Otto Ludwig, Graf von Bromberg
Generalinspekteur der Besatzungstruppen in Frankreich
