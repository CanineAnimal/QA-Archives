On The Captain:

A subordinate of the jumped-up child-empress? Well now. A soldier, caught up in the senseless warmongering wrought by his superiors, and yet staunch in his defense of his friends and allies... commendable, to a degree. I can honestly praise his selfless conduct and willingness to place others above his own wellbeing, though I do caution him on the blind adherence to orders. No matter how one loves their nation, rationalism should take precedence above all else. I cannot judge him as a leader, but I can certainly commend him as a solider. And as a person - it saddens me that so many officers, allegiance and nation notwithstanding, never seem to make the connection between the two.

On The Marshal:

Another soldier. This one at the helm of the nation she once served - a respectable rise. Her care for her people does show, but... it is misplaced, in a way. I will not badmouth her for dictatorial governance, for that would be hypocritical in the extreme. But to truly care for one's people means to offer them control of their own lives. Choices in their expression of values, their lifestyle. A drive to maintain traditionalism tends to work at cross-purposes with true care for the peoples' needs, going more in favour of establishing a set of values and social order that ultimately has no meaning - perhaps she has yet to realize this. Military strengthening is another respectable quality... marred, unfortunately, like the first. War glorification is most decidedly not the hallmark of a caring ruler. A nation should be prepared for war, yes, but to actively seek it is the height of folly. In short, my thoughts of the Marshal err towards the positive, though I implore her to consider the ramifications of certain stances she has deigned to assume. To restrict is to oppress, and oppression is naturally inimical to a supposed care for one's people.
