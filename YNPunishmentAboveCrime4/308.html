It is legal in Merulla. Extremely regulated, but legalized.
Basically, the rules for child labor are extremely strict and constantly monitored.

The minimum age for child labor is 13 years old.
Children aged 13 or over can only work if they are regular in a state education institution, in other words, in a Merullean public school.
The justification for the child to work is only accepted in cases of extreme need of the family due to poverty or if the objective is to teach the family business to the child.
In the case of teaching the family trade to the child, the child can only work under the supervision of the legal representative and if the business legally belongs to the family.
The child must be paid in full like any other employee of the company, however, the destination of the money varies according with the Child Labor Permission License. If it is the case of the license of extreme need, the government releases the value to the family, however, a federal fiscal agent is always keeping an eye on how this money is spent, to avoid exploiting and wasting the child's hard-earned money. On the other hand, if it is the case of teaching the family trade, the child's entire salary is confiscated by the government, which will place it in a trust fund in the child's name, which will be able to withdraw the entire accumulated amount without any tax deduction, as soon as he/she reaches 18 years old.

Any minor violation or infraction of the provisions of the Child Labor Act or any minor attempt to put a child to work without government permission, most likely will result in life imprisonment for those involved, in addition, the rest of the family will lose custody of the child if it is proven that they were colluding with the situation.

What's more, there are countless rules about child labor conditions, which also define a healthy and stimulating environment for the child, such as longer breaks for rest than for adult employees, at least one mandatory workshop for fun and distraction of the child and of course the total prohibition of physical punishment to the child. Again, if violated most likely lead to life imprisonment. With the exception of physical violence against the child, in this case, the death penalty can be applied to the child's aggressor.

In summary? Child labor is legalized and so heavily regulated so that it is not worth hiring children, even more so if you are thinking about cheap and easy labor, because they are the opposite of that.
90% of the children who work in Merulla have a family trade permit. The other 10%, who have the extreme need permit, usually work either with their own parents or at the school where they study as part of a state government initiative to welcome and help these children in poverty.
